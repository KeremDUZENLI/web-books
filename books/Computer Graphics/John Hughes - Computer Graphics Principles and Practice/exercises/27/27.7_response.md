### Answer 27.7
    
**Scenario:**
Imagine sunlight reflecting off a horizontal wet road (dielectric interface) and then hitting a driver's polarized sunglasses or a vertical glass store window.

**Artifact:**
1.  **First Bounce:** Reflection off the road at the Brewster angle is largely **horizontally polarized** (s-polarized). The vertical component (p-polarized) is transmitted into the road/water.
2.  **Second Interaction (Sunglasses):** Polarized sunglasses are designed to block horizontal polarization to reduce glare.
    * **Prediction (Unpolarized Assumption):** The graphics engine assumes the light arriving at the glasses is unpolarized. It computes standard transmission/reflection (e.g., 50% blocked).
    * **Reality:** The light is almost 100% horizontal. The glasses block 100% of it. The road glare should vanish.
    * **Artifact:** The rendered image shows glare on the road where the driver would see black/darkness.

**Second Interaction (Glass Window):** If the horizontally polarized light hits a vertical window at the Brewster angle, the polarization relative to the *window's* plane of incidence might be different (vertical relative to the window). The reflection properties would differ significantly from the unpolarized prediction.
