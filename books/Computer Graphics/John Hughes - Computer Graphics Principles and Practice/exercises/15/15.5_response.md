### Answer 15.5
    
1.  **Real number line:**
    The pixel centers are located at integer coordinates $k$. The open interval between two adjacent pixel centers $(k, k+1)$ has length 1. A segment of length $L < 1$ (e.g., $L = 0.999...$) placed precisely in this gap covers no centers.
    * Longest length: Just under 1.

2.  **Snapping to nearest integer:**
    Vertices snap to $V_{snap} = \text{round}(V)$.
    * If a segment has length $< 0.5$ and is centered between pixels (e.g., 0.5), both vertices might snap to different integers (0 and 1), creating a rasterized length of 1 covering pixels.
    * If vertices snap to the *same* integer (e.g., segment within $[0.6, 0.9]$ snaps to $[1, 1]$), the length is 0 and no pixels are covered.
    * Longest length covering no pixels: Any segment where vertices round to the same integer. The maximum distance between two points rounding to $k$ is bounded by the interval $[k - 0.5, k + 0.5)$, so length can approach 1.

3.  **8-bit subpixel precision (Fixed Point):**
    Grid spacing is $1/256$.
    * If vertices snap to the same subpixel grid point, length is 0.
    * If vertices snap to distinct grid points but fall within the capture region of a single pixel center without crossing the "diamond" or center-point threshold, they cover no pixels.
    * However, broadly, if the snapped subpixel coordinates lie strictly between pixel centers $k$ and $k+1$, the segment covers no pixels. The gap between diamond/center capture regions is effectively 1 pixel width.
    * Longest length: Just under 1.
