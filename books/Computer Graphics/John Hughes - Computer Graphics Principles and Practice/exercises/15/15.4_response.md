### Answer 15.4
    
Let $n$ be the number of primitives (triangles), $p$ be the number of pixels, and $a$ be the average area of a triangle in pixels.

1.  **Simple Rasterizer (Check every triangle against every pixel):**
    * Complexity: $O(n \cdot p)$
    * Preferred: Never (extremely inefficient).

2.  **Bounding-Box Rasterizer:**
    * Complexity: $O(n \cdot a)$ assuming $a$ reflects bounding box area.
    * Preferred: When triangles are small to medium-sized and cover localized screen regions. This is the standard hardware approach.

3.  **Hierarchical Rasterizer:**
    * Complexity: Approaches $O(n + p)$ for coverage, plus shading costs.
    * Preferred: When the scene contains large triangles covering many pixels (sparse sampling) or for very high-resolution displays to quickly eliminate empty regions.

4.  **Tiled/Chunking Rasterizer:**
    * Complexity: $O(n \cdot \text{tiles\_overlapped} + p)$.
    * Preferred: Memory-constrained environments (mobile/embedded) to maximize cache coherence and minimize memory bandwidth.

5.  **Scanline Rasterizer:**
    * Complexity: $O(n \log n + p)$ (sorting edges + filling spans).
    * Preferred: Software rendering where memory coherence for vertical iteration is poor, or historically when memory was too scarce for a full Z-buffer.
