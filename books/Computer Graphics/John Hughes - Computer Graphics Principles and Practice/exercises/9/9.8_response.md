### Answer 9.8
**(a) Range:**
Given $x \in [p, q]$, then $0 \le x-p \le q-p$.
Dividing by $q-p > 0$, we get $0 \le \frac{x-p}{q-p} \le 1$, so $0 \le \alpha(x) \le 1$.
Similarly, $0 \le q-x \le q-p$, and $\beta(x) = \frac{x-q}{p-q} = \frac{q-x}{q-p}$, so $0 \le \beta(x) \le 1$.

**(b) Sum:**
$\alpha(x) + \beta(x) = \frac{x-p}{q-p} + \frac{x-q}{p-q} = \frac{x-p}{q-p} - \frac{x-q}{q-p} = \frac{x - p - x + q}{q-p} = \frac{q-p}{q-p} = 1$.

**(c) Relation:**
Barycentric coordinates are ratios of lengths. $\alpha_{p',q'}$ is related to $\alpha_{p,q}$ by the linear transformation mapping $[p,q]$ to $[p',q']$. Specifically, if $T(x)$ maps $[p,q]$ to $[p',q']$, then $\alpha_{p,q}(x) = \alpha_{p',q'}(T(x))$.
