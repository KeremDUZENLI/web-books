### Answer 9.9
**(a)**
$f(P_i) = f_0 + (P_i - P_0) \cdot w$.
For $i=1$: $f(P_1) = f_0 + v_1 \cdot w \implies v_1 \cdot w = f_1 - f_0$.
For $i=2$: $f(P_2) = f_0 + v_2 \cdot w \implies v_2 \cdot w = f_2 - f_0$.

**(b)**
$S = [v_1, v_2]$. The matrix-vector product $S^T w$ results in a vector where the $i$-th component is the dot product of the $i$-th column of $S$ (which is $v_i$) with $w$.
Thus, $S^T w = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \cdot w \\ v_2 \cdot w \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} f_1 - f_0 \\ f_2 - f_0 \end{bmatrix}$.
Multiplying both sides by $S$ gives $S S^T w = S \begin{bmatrix} \Delta f_1 \\ \Delta f_2 \end{bmatrix}$.

**(c)**
This question likely contains a typo in the source text regarding dimensions (if $S$ is $3 \times 2$, $SS^T$ is $3 \times 3$ rank 2 and singular). Assuming the question refers to the Gram matrix $G = S^T S$ (or that $S$ is defined as rows), $S^T S$ is the matrix of dot products of the basis vectors. Since $v_1$ and $v_2$ are linearly independent (non-degenerate triangle), their Gram matrix is invertible.

$w = (S S^T)^{-1} S [\dots]$ (Text formula).
(Standard derivation for $w$ in the plane of the triangle would be $w = S(S^T S)^{-1} [\dots]$).

**(e)**
Substitute $w$ back into $f(P_i)$.
For $P_0$: $f(P_0) = f_0 + 0 = f_0$.
For $P_1$: $(P_1-P_0) \cdot w = v_1 \cdot w$. If $w$ satisfies $S^T w = \Delta f$, then $v_1 \cdot w = f_1 - f_0$. Thus $f(P_1) = f_0 + f_1 - f_0 = f_1$. Same for $P_2$.

**(f)**
$w' = w + \alpha n$.
$(P_i - P_0)$ is a vector in the plane of the triangle (a linear combination of $v_1, v_2$).
Since $n = v_1 \times v_2$, $n$ is orthogonal to the plane.
$(P_i - P_0) \cdot w' = (P_i - P_0) \cdot w + \alpha (P_i - P_0) \cdot n$.
The second term is zero.
Thus $f(P)$ is unchanged at the vertices (and all points in the plane).
