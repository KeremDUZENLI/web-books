### Answer 7.13

**(a)** With finite precision, a "random" ray might pass so close to a vertex that rounding errors make it unclear which side it passes on, or it might intersect an edge at a grazing angle. The probability of hitting a "problematic" zone is small but non-zero in floating point math.
**(b)** For a tiny quadrilateral centered at the origin, the angles subtended by vertices are small or degenerate relative to the test point $Q=(0,0)$. The ray directions to vertices are along axes. An arbitrary ray will pass very close to vertices relative to the machine epsilon.
**Sum-of-angles:** This method computes the sum of signed angles subtended by edges. For $Q=(0,0)$ inside the quad, the sum is exactly $2\pi$. This calculation is generally robust because it sums large angles ($90^\circ$) rather than testing minute intersection deviations.
