### Answer 25.5
    
**Argument for Absolute Value:**
Weights in the optimization roughly correspond to "stiffness" or "importance" of a feature. The mean curvature $H$ measures the deviation from flatness.
* **Ridges (Convex):** Have large positive $H$.
* **Valleys (Concave):** Have large negative $H$.
Both ridges and valleys are visual features that should be preserved. Using the signed value $H$ (specifically in a cumulative distribution $C(\kappa)$) would rank valleys as "low importance" (low weight) and ridges as "high importance," leading to asymmetric smoothing where valleys are flattened but ridges are preserved. Using $|H|$ treats both types of high-curvature features symmetrically, preserving all sharp details.

**Argument Against / Rationale for Unimportance:**
* **Nature of Noise:** If the algorithm is intended to smooth noise, and the noise is high-frequency oscillation, it adds both positive and negative curvature. However, if the underlying surface is mostly convex, the "features" are positive. If the mesh is generally convex, $|H| \approx H$, so the change is negligible.
* **Weight Function:** If the weights are derived from $H^2$ or a similar magnitude-based function in the actual implementation, the sign would wash out anyway.
* **Context:** In many modeling scenarios, the visual importance of convex features (silhouettes, bumps) often outweighs concave creases, so preserving positive curvature might be the primary design goal, making the distinction less critical in practice.
