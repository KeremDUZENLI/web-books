### Answer 20.2
    
**Proof by contradiction:**
Assume such a function $f(x,z)$ exists. The normal to the surface $y = f(x,z)$ is given by the gradient vector $[- \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, 1, - \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}]^T$, normalized.
The given normal field is $n(x,z) = \mathcal{S}([-z, 1, x]^T)$.
For these to match, the horizontal components must be proportional to the vertical component.
$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = z$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial z} = -x$.

Consider the change in $f$ along the closed curve $\gamma(t) = (\cos t, 0, \sin t)$ (the unit circle in the xz-plane).
The change in height $\Delta f$ traversing the loop is the path integral of the gradient:
$$ \Delta f = \oint \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \frac{dz}{dt} \right) dt $$
Substitute the derivatives and path parameterization:
$x = \cos t \implies dx = -\sin t dt$
$z = \sin t \implies dz = \cos t dt$
$$ \Delta f = \int_{0}^{2\pi} ( (\sin t)(-\sin t) + (-\cos t)(\cos t) ) dt $$
$$ \Delta f = \int_{0}^{2\pi} -(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t) dt = \int_{0}^{2\pi} -1 dt = -2\pi $$
For $f$ to be a valid function, $\Delta f$ around a closed loop must be 0. Since the integral is $-2\pi$, no such surface $f$ exists. The normal field has "curl" and represents a "spiral staircase" structure that never closes.
