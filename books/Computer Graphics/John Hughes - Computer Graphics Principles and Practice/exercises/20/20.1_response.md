### Answer 20.1
    
**Analysis of the modified inner product:**
The Phong specular term typically relies on $(r \cdot v)^n$, where $r$ is the reflection vector and $v$ is the view vector. Modifying the inner product to $u^T M w$ introduces anisotropy.
1.  **Matrix Construction:**
    $M = V D V^T$.
    $V = [\mathbf{t}_1, \mathbf{t}_2, \mathbf{n}]$. This is an orthonormal frame at the surface.
    $D = \text{Diag}(s, t, 1)$.
    The inner product becomes $(u^T V) D (V^T w)$. This transforms vectors $u$ and $w$ into the tangent space basis, scales the components by $s, t, 1$, and takes the dot product.

2.  **Effect on Highlights:**
    If $s < t$, the "dot product" calculation is damped more in the $\mathbf{t}_1$ direction than the $\mathbf{t}_2$ direction.
    A standard specular highlight is circular (isotropic).
    With $s \neq t$, the highlight becomes elliptical.
    * If $s \ll 1$ and $t \approx 1$, the highlight stretches along the direction of $\mathbf{t}_1$ (where the sensitivity to angle change is reduced), creating streaks perpendicular to the scaling direction. This mimics the microfacet distribution of brushed metal, where grooves (along $\mathbf{t}_2$) cause scattering in a plane perpendicular to them.

**Conclusion:**
By setting $s$ and $t$ to different values (e.g., $s=0.1, t=1.0$), the specular highlight stretches into a streak, simulating the anisotropic reflection characteristic of brushed metal surfaces or hair.
