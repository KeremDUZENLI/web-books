### Answer 20.3
    
**(a) Rendering Program:**
Implement a ray tracer or pixel shader. For ground point $(x, 0, z)$:
`color = (floor(x) + floor(z)) % 2 == 0 ? 0.85 : 0.15;`

**(b) Band Limits:**
Project a unit square at distance $z$.
* Perspective projection scales lengths by $1/z$.
* A texture feature of size 1 projects to screen size $s \approx c/z$ (where $c$ depends on resolution and FOV).
* The frequency in texture space is 1 cycle/unit.
* The frequency in screen space is $z/c$ cycles/pixel.
* Nyquist limit is 0.5 cycles/pixel.
* Max texture frequency $\omega_{max} = 0.5 \cdot (c/z)$.
* Cutoff frequency $\omega_0$ for the partial sum decreases linearly as $z$ increases.

**(c) Fourier Series Implementation:**
Function $S(x)$ is a square wave.
$\overline{S}(x, \omega_0)$ truncates the series.
At large $z$, $\omega_0$ becomes small, keeping only the first term (sine wave) or just the DC component (average gray).
This reduces Moiré patterns but introduces ringing (Gibbs phenomenon) where the truncation is abrupt.

**(d) Evaluation:**
Direct truncation multiplies the spectrum by a box function (Rect).
Multiplication by Rect in frequency domain $\leftrightarrow$ Convolution with Sinc in spatial domain.
Sinc has infinite support and oscillates, causing "ripples" or ringing near sharp transitions (checkerboard edges).

**(e) Fejer Kernel:**
Replace the box filter with a tent filter (Fejer kernel) in the frequency domain.
$Tent(\omega) \leftrightarrow Sinc^2(x)$.
$Sinc^2$ is non-negative and decays faster than Sinc.
Using the Fejer summation (averaging partial sums) eliminates the overshoot of the Gibbs phenomenon, producing a smoother, blurred transition at the horizon instead of ringing artifacts.
