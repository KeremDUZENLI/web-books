### Answer 21.1
    
**Implementation:**
In this variant, the axis of rotation is $v = (Q-C) \times (P-C)$, where $P$ and $Q$ are points on the image plane (mouse positions) and $C$ is the object center in 3D. The rotation angle $\theta$ is $k \cdot ||Q-P||$.

**Disadvantages:**
1.  **Spinning about the View Axis:** To spin the object around the eye-to-object axis (the "roll" axis), the user needs to generate a rotation vector parallel to the view direction. In this model, the axis is derived from the cross product of vectors in the image plane (assuming $P, Q$ are on the film plane) or vectors relating to $C$. If $C$ is projected to the image plane, dragging perpendicular to the radius vector $P-C_{proj}$ generates a roll. However, if the drag passes *through* $C$, the cross product is zero, causing a singularity.
2.  **Unintuitive Mapping:** The proportionality constant $k$ is arbitrary. Unlike the virtual sphere where the rotation is geometrically tied to the surface curvature, this linear mapping might feel "slippery" or disconnected if $k$ doesn't match the user's expectation of how much pixel motion equals how much rotation.
3.  **Lack of Feedback:** There is no visual "sphere" to guide the user on where the rotation behavior changes (though this method claims to avoid the "off-sphere" edge case, it lacks the affordance of the sphere boundaries).
