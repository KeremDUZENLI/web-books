### Answer 10.4
**(a)** If $Q-P$ and $R-P$ were linearly dependent, then $R-P = c(Q-P)$ for some scalar $c$. This implies $R = P + c(Q-P)$, meaning $P, Q, R$ lie on the same line. This contradicts the hypothesis that they are noncollinear.
**(b)** If $A, B, C$ were collinear, then $C-A = k(B-A)$, implying $v_2 = k v_1$. This contradicts the hypothesis that $v_1, v_2$ are linearly independent.
**(c)**
* **(i) to (ii):** Let point $P = P_1$. Vectors $v_1 = P_2-P_1, v_2 = P_3-P_1, v_3 = P_4-P_1$.
* **(ii) to (i):** Points $P_1 = P, P_2 = P+v_1, P_3 = P+v_2, P_4 = P+v_3$.
* **Third Version:** Two points $P, Q$ and two vectors $u, v$. Equivalence requires $Q-P, u, v$ to be linearly independent.
