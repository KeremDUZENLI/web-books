### Answer 26.13
    
**Derivation:**
Planck's Law: $R(f, T) = \frac{2 h f^3}{c^2} \frac{1}{e^{hf/kT} - 1}$.
Let $u = \frac{hf}{kT}$. Then $f = \frac{ukT}{h}$.
Substitute $f$:
$R \propto f^3 / (e^u - 1) \propto u^3 T^3 / (e^u - 1)$.
Since $T$ is fixed for the maximization with respect to $f$, we maximize $S(u) = \frac{u^3}{e^u - 1}$.

**Maximization Condition:**
Differentiate $S(u)$ wrt $u$ and set to 0.
$S'(u) = \frac{3u^2(e^u - 1) - u^3(e^u)}{(e^u - 1)^2} = 0$.
$3(e^u - 1) - u e^u = 0$.
$3e^u - 3 - u e^u = 0$.
$e^u(3 - u) = 3$.
$3 - u = 3e^{-u}$.
$(3 - u)e^u = 3$? No.
Approximate solution for $u$:
If $u$ is large (Wien limit), $e^u \gg 1$, $S(u) \approx u^3 e^{-u}$.
Derivative: $3u^2 e^{-u} - u^3 e^{-u} = 0 \implies 3 - u = 0 \implies u = 3$.
So peak is at $u \approx 3$. (Exact solution is $u \approx 2.821$).

**Linearity:**
$f_{peak} = \frac{u_{peak} k T}{h}$.
Since $u_{peak}, k, h$ are constants, $f_{peak} \propto T$.
This is linear in T.
