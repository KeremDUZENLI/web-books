### Answer 26.11
    
A general plane wave traveling in x has components:
$E_y = A_y \cos(kx - \omega t + \delta_y)$
$E_z = A_z \cos(kx - \omega t + \delta_z)$

We can decompose the vector phasor $\mathbf{A} = (A_y e^{i\delta_y}, A_z e^{i\delta_z})$.
Circular polarization requires equal magnitudes and $\pm \pi/2$ phase difference.
Linear polarization requires in-phase (or $\pi$ phase) components.
Any complex vector in 2D can be written as a sum of a linear vector (real times phase) and a circular vector.
Specifically, elliptical polarization (the general case) is the superposition of linear and circular states.
(The rigorous algebraic proof involves solving for the coefficients of the linear and circular basis vectors that sum to the given $A_y, A_z$ and phases).
