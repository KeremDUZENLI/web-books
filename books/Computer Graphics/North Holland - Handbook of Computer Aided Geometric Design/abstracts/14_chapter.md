# Chapter 14: Multiresolution Techniques

---

## Main Idea

This chapter details algorithms that decompose geometric objects into a hierarchy of global shapes and local details, enabling efficient processing, storage, and transmission. It establishes the theoretical framework of multiresolution analysis using wavelets and the lifting scheme, and extends these concepts from regular functions to arbitrary 3D surface meshes using subdivision and mesh decimation.

---

## Keywords

**Multiresolution Analysis:**
A mathematical framework for representing a function (or surface) at multiple levels of detail, separating the low-frequency "coarse" shape from the high-frequency "fine" details.

**Wavelet:**
A basis function that encodes the difference (detail information) between two successive levels of resolution. Unlike standard basis functions, wavelets typically have a mean value of zero (vanishing moments).

**Lifting Scheme:**
A method to construct wavelets by factoring the transform into simple "prediction" and "update" steps. It allows for in-place computation and is easily adaptable to irregular meshes.

**Mesh Decimation:**
An algorithm that reduces the complexity of a polygon mesh by iteratively removing vertices or collapsing edges, creating a "fine-to-coarse" hierarchy.

**Progressive Mesh:**
A representation that stores a coarse base mesh and a stream of refinement operations (vertex splits). This allows a 3D model to be reconstructed smoothly from low to high detail.

**Subdivision Connectivity:**
A specific mesh topology where a surface is generated by repeatedly refining a base mesh. This regular structure is required for standard wavelet transforms but can be approximated for arbitrary meshes via remeshing.

---

## Formulas

**Refinement (Subdivision)**

- **Formula:** $c_{i}^{\prime} = \sum_{j} \alpha_{i-2j} c_{j}$
- **Meaning:** Computes the control points on a finer level $c'$ as a weighted average of points on a coarser level $c$.
- **Use in graphics:** The standard operation for generating smooth surfaces from coarse polygons.

**Detail Coefficient (Prediction Error)**

- **Formula:** $d_{i} = c_{2i+1}^{\prime} - \sum_{j} \alpha_{2i+1-2j} c_{2j}^{\prime}$
- **Meaning:** Calculates the "detail" $d_i$ as the difference between the actual data and the position predicted by the subdivision rule.
- **Use in graphics:** Captures the geometric texture or noise that is removed during smoothing or compression.

**Reconstruction**

- **Formula:** $f' = \sum_{i} c_{i} \phi(\cdot-i) + \sum_{i} d_{i} \psi(\cdot-i)$
- **Meaning:** Reconstructs the high-resolution signal $f'$ by adding the details (weighted by wavelets $\psi$) back onto the coarse approximation (weighted by scaling functions $\phi$).
- **Use in graphics:** Used to restore a high-quality 3D model after editing or transmission.

---

## Practical Use

**Multiresolution Editing**
Designers can modify the overall shape of an object (like bending a character's arm) on a coarse level, and the system automatically reconstructs the fine surface details (like wrinkles or texture) on top of the new shape.

**Geometry Compression**
By converting a model into a multiresolution format, insignificant detail coefficients can be discarded. This significantly reduces file size while maintaining the visual quality of the 3D object.

**Level of Detail (LOD) Rendering**
Complex scenes can be rendered faster by using coarse versions of objects when they are far away from the camera and progressively adding detail as they get closer.
