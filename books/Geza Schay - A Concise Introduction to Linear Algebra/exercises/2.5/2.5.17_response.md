### Answer 2.5.17

**a. Permutation Matrices ($3 \times 3$):**
1.  $I$ (Identity). Inv: $I$.
2.  Swap 1-2: $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$. Inv: Self.
3.  Swap 1-3: $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Inv: Self.
4.  Swap 2-3: $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Inv: Self.
5.  Cycle 1->2->3->1: $P = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Inv: $P^T = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$.
6.  Cycle 1->3->2->1: $P = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Inv: $P^T$ (The matrix in 5).

**b. $PA$:**
$P$ has a 1 in column $j$ of row $i$.
$(PA)_{ik} = \sum_r P_{ir} A_{rk} = P_{ij} A_{jk} = A_{jk}$.
The $i$-th row of $PA$ is the $j$-th row of $A$.
This is exactly the permutation defined by $P$.

**c. $BP$:**
Column permutation. $BP$ is $B$ with columns permuted according to $P^T$ (inverse permutation).
