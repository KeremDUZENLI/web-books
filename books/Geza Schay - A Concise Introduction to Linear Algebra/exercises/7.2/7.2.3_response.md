### Answer 7.2.3

**Proof:**
Let $A$ be symmetric. By the Spectral Theorem, it is orthogonally diagonalizable: $A = Q \Lambda Q^T$ with $Q$ orthogonal ($Q^T = Q^{-1}$) and $\Lambda$ real diagonal.
Given eigenvalues $\lambda_i \ge 0$, we can define $\sqrt{\Lambda} = \text{diag}(\sqrt{\lambda_1}, \dots, \sqrt{\lambda_n})$.

Define $\sqrt{A} = Q \sqrt{\Lambda} Q^T$.

1.  **Exists:**
    $$ \sqrt{A}\sqrt{A} = (Q \sqrt{\Lambda} Q^T)(Q \sqrt{\Lambda} Q^T) = Q \sqrt{\Lambda} (\sqrt{\Lambda}) Q^T = Q \Lambda Q^T = A $$
2.  **Symmetric:**
    $$ (\sqrt{A})^T = (Q \sqrt{\Lambda} Q^T)^T = (Q^T)^T (\sqrt{\Lambda})^T Q^T = Q \sqrt{\Lambda} Q^T = \sqrt{A} $$
    (Since diagonal matrices are symmetric).
