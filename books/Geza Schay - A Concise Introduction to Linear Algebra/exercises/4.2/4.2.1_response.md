### Answer 4.2.1

**Proof:**
We assume $T: W \to V$ is linear.
1.  **Zero Vector:** $T(\mathbf{0}_W)$ must be defined.
    $T(0\mathbf{x}) = 0T(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}_V$.
    For $0\mathbf{x}$ to be in the domain $W$, $\mathbf{0}_W \in W$.
2.  **Closure under Addition:**
    $T(\mathbf{x}_1 + \mathbf{x}_2)$ must be defined.
    Usually, linearity is defined *on* a vector space. If $T$ is linear on a subset $W$, it implies for any $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in W$, $T(\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y})$ makes sense, so $\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y} \in W$.
3.  **Closure under Scalar Multiplication:**
    $T(c\mathbf{x})$ defined $\implies c\mathbf{x} \in W$.
Thus $W$ must be a subspace.
