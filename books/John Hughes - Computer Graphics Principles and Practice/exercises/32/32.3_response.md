### Answer 32.3

**(a) Predictions:**

1. **Direct Lighting:** Storing photons for direct lighting is redundant and inefficient compared to standard shadow rays. Shadow rays test visibility to a known light source analytically. Photon mapping relies on density estimation; unless the photon count is massive, the direct lighting estimate will be noisy (blotchy) and edges of shadows will be blurred. A final gather would help smooth out the blotchiness but is computationally expensive compared to direct next-event estimation.
2. **Specular Reflections:** Storing photons on specular surfaces (mirrors) is generally useless for the standard photon map because the probability of an eye ray hitting the exact point required to see a specular reflection of a specific photon is zero. The photon map density estimation blurs radiance over an area; applying this to a perfect mirror would result in a blurry reflection (incorrectly simulating a glossy surface) or would require infinite density to resolve the sharp reflection. For caustics (specular-to-diffuse), a separate photon map is useful, but for viewing the specular surface itself, ray tracing is superior.

**(b) Implementation Results:**

* **Direct Lighting:** Images appear splotchy. Sharp shadow boundaries become fuzzy blobs unless millions of photons are used.
* **Specular:** Reflections look foggy or blurred. High-frequency details in reflections are lost. Final gathering essentially reverts to path tracing (sending rays out) but samples the blurred photon map, so the result is a smooth but incorrect (blurred) reflection.
