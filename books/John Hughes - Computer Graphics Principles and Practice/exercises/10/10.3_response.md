### Answer 10.3
**(a)** $V$ is defined as an orthogonal matrix in the SVD, meaning its columns are orthonormal. Therefore, $V^T V = I$.
**(b)** $V^T v_i$ is the $i$-th column of $V^T V$. Since $V^T V = I$, the $i$-th column is the standard basis vector $e_i$ (1 at index $i$, 0 elsewhere).
**(c)** $D(V^T v_i) = D e_i = d_i e_i$ (where $d_i$ is the $i$-th diagonal element).
**(d)** $M v_i = U (D V^T v_i) = U (d_i e_i) = d_i (U e_i) = d_i u_i$ (where $u_i$ is the $i$-th column of $U$).
**(e)** $M' v_i = (\sum_{k=1}^n d_k u_k v_k^T) v_i = \sum d_k u_k (v_k^T v_i)$. Since $v_k$ are orthonormal, $v_k^T v_i = \delta_{ki}$. The sum collapses to $d_i u_i$.
**(f)** The vectors $v_i$ are columns of an orthogonal matrix, so they form an orthonormal basis for $R^n$ and are linearly independent.
**(g)** Since $M$ and $M'$ map the basis vectors $v_i$ to identical outputs ($d_i u_i$), they define the same linear transformation.
**(h)** Two matrices representing the same linear transformation in the same basis must be identical. Thus $M' = M$.
