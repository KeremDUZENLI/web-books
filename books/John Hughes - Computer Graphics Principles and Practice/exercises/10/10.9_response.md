### Answer 10.9
**(a)** If $\alpha_Q = 0$, then $\alpha_P v_P + \alpha_R v_R = 0$. Since $P \neq R$, $v_P$ and $v_R$ are linearly independent (as points), which implies $\alpha_P = \alpha_R = 0$. This contradicts the premise that not all $\alpha$ are zero. Thus $\alpha_Q \neq 0$.
**(b)** $\alpha_Q v_Q = -\alpha_P v_P - \alpha_R v_R \implies v_Q = -\frac{\alpha_P}{\alpha_Q} v_P - \frac{\alpha_R}{\alpha_Q} v_R$.
Sum of coefficients: $-\frac{\alpha_P}{\alpha_Q} - \frac{\alpha_R}{\alpha_Q} = \frac{-(\alpha_P+\alpha_R)}{\alpha_Q}$. Since $\sum \alpha = 0$, $\alpha_P+\alpha_R = -\alpha_Q$. Sum $= \frac{-(-\alpha_Q)}{\alpha_Q} = 1$.
Since $Q$ is an affine combination of $P$ and $R$, they are collinear.
**(c)** If $P=Q$, then $1 \cdot v_P + (-1) \cdot v_Q + 0 \cdot v_R = 0$ is a non-trivial linear dependence. Collinearity is trivially satisfied as any two points define a line.
