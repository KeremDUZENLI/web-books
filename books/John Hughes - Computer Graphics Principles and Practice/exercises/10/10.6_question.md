### Exercise 10.6
An image (i.e., an array of grayscale values between 0 and 1, say) can be thought of as a large matrix, M (indeed, this is how we usually represent images in our programs). Use a linear algebra library to compute the SVD $M=UDV^{T}$ of some image M. According to the decomposition theorem described in Exercise 10.3, this describes the image as a sum of outer products of many vectors. If we replace the last 90% of the diagonal entries of D with zeroes to get a new matrix $D^{\prime}$, then the product $M^{\prime}=UD^{\prime}V^{T}$ deletes 90% of the terms in this sum of outer products. In doing so, however, it deletes the smallest 90% of the terms. Display $M^{\prime}$ and compare it to M. Experiment with values other than 90%. At what level do the two images become indistinguishable? You may encounter values less than 0 and greater than 1 during the process described in this exercise. You should simply clamp these values to the interval [0, 1].
