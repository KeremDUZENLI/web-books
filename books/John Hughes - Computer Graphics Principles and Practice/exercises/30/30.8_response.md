### Answer 30.8
    
**(a) Density Transformation**
We transform random variable $X$ via $X = f(T)$? No, the prompt implies $Y$ is distributed according to the transformed density.
Let's check if $g(t) = e(f(t))f'(t)$ integrates to 1.
$$ \int_a^b e(f(t)) f'(t) dt $$
Let $u = f(t)$. Then $du = f'(t) dt$.
Limits: $f(a) = 0, f(b) = 1$ (since $f$ is bijective to $[0,1]$).
$$ \int_0^1 e(u) du = 1 $$
(Since $e$ is a valid density on [0,1]).
So yes, it is a PDF.

**(b) Relationship**
Given $Y$ has density $p_Y(t) = e(f(t))f'(t)$.
$X$ has density $p_X(x) = e(x)$.
Relationship $x_0 = f(t_0)$.
$$ Pr\{t_0-\epsilon \le Y \le t_0+\epsilon\} \approx p_Y(t_0) \cdot 2\epsilon $$
$$ = e(f(t_0)) f'(t_0) \cdot 2\epsilon = e(x_0) f'(t_0) \cdot 2\epsilon $$
$$ Pr\{x_0-\epsilon \le X \le x_0+\epsilon\} \approx p_X(x_0) \cdot 2\epsilon = e(x_0) \cdot 2\epsilon $$
Ratio:
$$ \frac{Pr(Y \text{ near } t_0)}{Pr(X \text{ near } x_0)} = f'(t_0) $$
The probability mass is scaled by the derivative (Jacobian) of the transformation function $f$. This is the standard change-of-variables rule for probabilities.
