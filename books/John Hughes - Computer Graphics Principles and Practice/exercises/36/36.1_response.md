### Answer 36.1
    
To generate $O(nk)$ intersections using the Sutherland-Hodgman algorithm, the subject polygon must intersect the clip boundaries as frequently as possible.

**Construction:**
1.  **Boundary Polygon ($B$):** Let the boundary polygon be a regular convex $k$-gon centered at the origin (e.g., approximating a circle or a unit square).
2.  **Input Polygon ($P$):** Let the input polygon be a nonconvex "star" or "gear" shape with $n$ vertices, also centered at the origin.
    * Construct $P$ such that it has $n/2$ "spikes" (vertices far from the origin) and $n/2$ "valleys" (vertices close to the origin), alternating.
    * The radius of the spikes ($R_{max}$) must be significantly larger than the maximum extent of the boundary $B$.
    * The radius of the valleys ($R_{min}$) must be significantly smaller than the minimum extent (apothem) of the boundary $B$.

**Analysis:**
The Sutherland-Hodgman algorithm clips the subject polygon $P$ against each of the $k$ infinite lines defined by the edges of $B$ sequentially.

* Consider a single clip line $L$ from the boundary $B$. Because $P$ has $n/2$ spikes that extend far beyond $B$ (and thus beyond $L$) and $n/2$ valleys that lie deep inside $B$ (and thus inside $L$), the line $L$ will intersect the edges of $P$ roughly $n$ times (specifically, passing through every spike/valley pair).
* This generates $O(n)$ intersection points for *one* boundary edge.
* Since the input polygon is effectively a high-frequency wave oscillating across the boundary, this high intersection count is preserved (or only slightly reduced) for subsequent clip lines, provided the star is dense enough.
* Summing over the $k$ clipping stages, the total number of intersection points computed and inserted into the intermediate vertex lists is $\sum_{i=1}^{k} O(n) = O(nk)$.
