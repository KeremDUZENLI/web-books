### Answer 28.4
    
**Procedure:**
1.  Let $P(\lambda) = (X(\lambda), 1, Z(\lambda))$ derived from standard observer tables ($\bar{x}, \bar{y}, \bar{z}$) normalized such that $Y=1$.
    Specifically, for a monospectral light $\lambda$, the chromaticity is $(x_\lambda, y_\lambda)$.
    $X = x_\lambda/y_\lambda$, $Z = (1 - x_\lambda - y_\lambda)/y_\lambda$.
2.  **XYZ Distance:** $D_{XYZ}(\lambda) = \sqrt{(X_{\lambda+1}-X_\lambda)^2 + (1-1)^2 + (Z_{\lambda+1}-Z_\lambda)^2}$.
    This plot typically peaks where the color matching functions change rapidly (blue/red ends) and is small in the green.
3.  **$L^*u^*v^*$ Distance:** Convert points to $L^*u^*v^*$ (where $L^*=100$).
    $D_{Luv}(\lambda) = \sqrt{(u^*_{\lambda+1}-u^*_\lambda)^2 + (v^*_{\lambda+1}-v^*_\lambda)^2}$.
    Since $L^*$ is constant, this measures chromaticity distance scaled by intensity.

**Observations:**
* The $L^*u^*v^*$ distance represents the discriminability of spectral colors.
* **Largest difference:** Typically around 500-520 nm (blue-green) and the red/violet ends where hue changes rapidly.
* **Smallest difference:** In the yellow-green range (~550-580 nm) where the spectral locus is relatively straight and perceptually compressed.
