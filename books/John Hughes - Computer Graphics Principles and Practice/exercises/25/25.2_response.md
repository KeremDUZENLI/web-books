### Answer 25.2
    
The vertices are placed at the standard basis vectors of $\mathbb{R}^n$: vertex $m$ is at $e_m = (0, \dots, 1, \dots, 0)$.
Any point $P$ in the embedded triangle $T_1 = (i, j, k)$ is a convex combination:
$P = c_i e_i + c_j e_j + c_k e_k$ with $c_i + c_j + c_k = 1$ and $c \ge 0$.
Since the basis vectors are linearly independent, this representation is unique.

**(a) Disjoint Sets:**
Let $P \in T_1$ and $Q \in T_2$.
$P = \sum_{m \in S_1} c_m e_m$ and $Q = \sum_{r \in S_2} d_r e_r$.
If $P = Q$, the coefficients of the basis vectors must be identical.
If $S_1 \cap S_2 = \emptyset$, then for any index $m \in S_1$, the coefficient in $Q$ is 0 (since $m \notin S_2$). Thus $c_m$ must be 0.
Since $\sum c_m = 1$, this is impossible. Thus, no intersection exists.

**(b) Single Index Intersection:**
Let $S_1 \cap S_2 = \{p\}$.
Equating coefficients for $P = Q$:
For all $m \in S_1 \setminus \{p\}$, $c_m$ must equal the coefficient in $Q$ (which is 0). So $c_m = 0$.
The only non-zero coefficient allowed for $P$ is $c_p$.
Since $\sum c = 1$, we must have $c_p = 1$.
Thus $P = 1 \cdot e_p = v_p$. The intersection is exactly the vertex $p$.

**(c) Two Index Intersection:**
Let $S_1 \cap S_2 = \{p, q\}$.
Non-shared indices must have coefficients of 0.
$P$ must be a convex combination of only $e_p$ and $e_q$.
$P = c_p e_p + c_q e_q$ with $c_p + c_q = 1$.
This is the definition of the line segment (embedded edge) connecting $v_p$ and $v_q$.
