### Answer 19.5
    
**(a) Well-defined (Uniqueness):**
The Sampling Theorem states that the map from the space of band-limited functions (limit $\omega_0=1/2$) to the space of samples $l^2(\mathbb{Z})$ is a bijection (isomorphism). Given a set of samples, there is exactly one function in this band-limited space that passes through them.

**(b) Linearity:**
Let $S$ be the sampling operator and $R$ be reconstruction. Convolution is linear:
$(s_1 + s_2) * sinc = (s_1 * sinc) + (s_2 * sinc)$.
Thus $R(s_1 + s_2) = R(s_1) + R(s_2)$.

**(c) Translation Equivariance:**
Let $s_3(n) = s_1(n+k)$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_3(x) &= \sum s_3(n) \text{sinc}(x-n) \\
&= \sum s_1(n+k) \text{sinc}(x-n)
\end{aligned}
$$
Let $m = n+k \Rightarrow n = m-k$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_3(x) &= \sum s_1(m) \text{sinc}(x-(m-k)) \\
&= \sum s_1(m) \text{sinc}((x+k)-m) \\
&= f_1(x+k)
\end{aligned}
$$

**(d) Impulse Response:**
If $s(0)=1$ and $s(n)=0$ for $n \ne 0$:
$$
f(x) = \sum s(n) \text{sinc}(x-n) = 1 \cdot \text{sinc}(x-0) + 0 = \text{sinc}(x)
$$

**(e) Conclusion:**
Any discrete signal $s$ can be written as a sum of shifted impulses: $s(n) = \sum_k s(k) \delta(n-k)$.
By linearity and equivariance:
$$
f(x) = \sum_k s(k) \text{sinc}(x-k) = (s * \text{sinc})(x)
$$
