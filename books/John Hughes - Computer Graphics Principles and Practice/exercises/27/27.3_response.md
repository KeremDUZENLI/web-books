### Answer 27.3
    
**Explanation:**
The blurring is due to **glossy reflection** (microfacet scattering), not primarily the Fresnel effect.
1.  **Finite Beam Width:** The polished wood is not a perfect mirror; it has microscopic roughness. Light rays from a single point on the book spine hit the wood and scatter into a small cone of directions (a lobe) rather than a single ray.
2.  **Distance Scaling:**
    * **Bottom of spine:** The book is in contact with the wood. The distance the reflected rays travel from the object to the surface is zero. The spread of the reflection cone is negligible at the point of contact, so the image is sharp.
    * **Top of spine:** The light travels from the top of the spine to the wood surface (distance $d$). Over this distance, the cone of scattered rays spreads out. By the time the reflection reaches the eye, rays from adjacent points on the book overlap, causing blur. The blur radius is proportional to the distance from the reflector.

**Test:**
Place a distinct pattern (e.g., text) on a card. Hold it touching the mirror; it looks sharp. Slowly lift the card; the reflection becomes progressively blurrier as the distance increases.
