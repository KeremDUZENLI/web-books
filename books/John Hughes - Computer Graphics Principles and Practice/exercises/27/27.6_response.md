**Data:**
* **Aluminum:** $n_2 \approx 1.44$ (Given). $n_1 = 1.0$.
* **Magnesium Oxide:** $n_2 = 1.74$. $n_1 = 1.0$.

**1. Normal Incidence Reflectance $R_F(0)$:**
Formula: $R_F(0) = \left( \frac{n_2 - n_1}{n_2 + n_1} \right)^2$.
* **Aluminum:** $\left( \frac{0.44}{2.44} \right)^2 \approx 0.0325$.
* **MgO:** $\left( \frac{0.74}{2.74} \right)^2 \approx 0.0729$.

**2. Schlick Approximation:**
$R_{Schlick}(\theta) = R_F(0) + (1 - R_F(0))(1 - \cos\theta)^5$.

**3. Comparison:**
* **Aluminum:** The value $n=1.44$ provided for Aluminum leads to a very low $R_F(0)$ (3.2%), behaving like a weak dielectric (glass). Real aluminum is a metal with high reflectance ($>90\%$), which requires a complex index of refraction ($k > 0$). Using the Schlick approximation with the *given* $n=1.44$ yields a dielectric curve, which is physically incorrect for real metal but mathematically consistent with the input. If one used the *real* reflectance of Aluminum ($R \approx 0.9$), Schlick would approximate the metal curve well (flat high reflectance).
* **MgO:** As a dielectric, the Fresnel equations (and derived $R_F(0)$) are physically accurate. Schlick is designed to approximate this curve. The error is generally small ($< 1\%$) for standard dielectrics.

**Conclusion:** Schlick works well for MgO. For Aluminum, using $n=1.44$ (without extinction $k$) gives a physically wrong baseline, but Schlick successfully approximates the *mathematical* Fresnel curve for that specific $n$.
