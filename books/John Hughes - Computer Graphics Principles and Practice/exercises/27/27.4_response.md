### Answer 27.4
    
**(a) Separation of Color and Reflectance:**
They are likely separated to distinguish between **material chromaticity** (the RGB color) and **physical intensity/albedo** (the scalar reflectance). This allows:
1.  **Energy Conservation:** Easier to ensure $\rho_d + \rho_s \le 1$ by checking scalar coefficients rather than arbitrary RGB vectors.
2.  **Texture Mapping:** Artists can map "brightness" (reflectance) and "pattern" (diffuse color) separately.

**(b) Specular Exponent and Wavelength:**
The specular exponent ($n_s$ in Phong, or $m$ in Cook-Torrance) models **surface roughness** (the geometric distribution of microfacets). Geometry is generally macroscopic compared to light wavelength (geometric optics approximation) and affects all wavelengths equally. Therefore, the spread of the highlight depends on the physical surface shape, not the light color.

**(c) Wavelength-dependent Exponent:**
If Red has $n_s = 3$ (rougher/wider lobe) and Green/Blue have $n_s = 5$ (sharper/narrower lobe):
* **Center:** All three components are reflected strongly. The center of the highlight would appear white.
* **Edges:** The Green and Blue components fall off faster (narrower lobe). The Red component falls off slower (wider lobe).
* **Result:** The specular highlight would have a **white center surrounded by a reddish halo**, as the red reflection persists at angles where blue and green have faded.
