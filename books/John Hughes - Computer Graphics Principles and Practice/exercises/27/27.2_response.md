### Answer 27.2
    
**Feature Description:**
In photographs of reflections on water, the "glitter path" forms a wedge. It is narrow near the horizon (or the object, if distant) and widens towards the observer.

**Physical Explanation:**
This phenomenon is explained by **microfacet theory** and the probability distribution of surface slopes (waves).
1.  **Reflection Condition:** For a point on the water to reflect the light source to the eye, the local surface normal must bisect the angle between the light vector and the view vector.
2.  **Wave Slope:** The water surface is not flat; it consists of waves with varying slopes (microfacets). The probability of finding a facet with a specific slope follows a distribution (e.g., Gaussian).
3.  **Grazing Angles (Horizon):** When looking near the horizon, the view vector is shallow. A small range of wave slopes is sufficient to reflect the light to the eye.
4.  **Near Viewer:** As the view looks down at the water closer to the observer, the required normal for reflection changes. Due to perspective and the geometry of reflection, a wider range of lateral positions on the water surface provides normals that reflect the source to the eye. The reflection widens because the "cone" of normals capable of reflection, when projected onto the water surface from the viewer's perspective, intersects a wider swath of the water closer to the viewer.
