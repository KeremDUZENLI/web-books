### Answer 8.3

Adding a triangle can create non-manifold conditions in four ways:

1.  **Two cones meeting at a vertex:** Adding a triangle that touches an existing mesh only at a single vertex $V$ (where $V$ is already interior to the existing mesh or part of a surface). This creates a "bowtie" shape where the neighborhood of $V$ is not a topological disk.
2.  **Non-manifold edge (3+ faces):** Adding a triangle that shares an edge with an existing mesh, where that edge is already shared by two triangles. This results in an edge having 3 adjacent faces.
3.  **Two cones meeting at an edge:** Adding a triangle that connects two previously disjoint parts of the mesh along an edge? (Usually covered by case 2). Or, closing a hole such that a vertex becomes singular?
4.  **Closing a loop non-manifoldly:** Adding a face that connects vertices in a way that creates a Mobius strip configuration or connects a vertex to itself?

*Note: The text refers to "four changes described in the nonmanifold vertex representation" specifically regarding the directed-edge structure updates or specific topological events. Standard non-manifold events are usually: (1) isolated vertex, (2) dangling edge, (3) singular vertex (bowtie), (4) singular edge (3+ faces).*
