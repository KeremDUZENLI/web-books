### Answer 18.1
    
(a) By definition, the space $L^2$ consists precisely of those functions $f$ for which the integral of the square is finite ($\int f^2 < \infty$). Therefore, the length $||f|| = \sqrt{\int f^2}$ is necessarily finite.

(b) Expansion of the norm:
$$
\begin{aligned}
||f+g||^2 - ||f||^2 - ||g||^2 &= \langle f+g, f+g \rangle - \langle f,f \rangle - \langle g,g \rangle \\
&= (\langle f,f \rangle + \langle f,g \rangle + \langle g,f \rangle + \langle g,g \rangle) - \langle f,f \rangle - \langle g,g \rangle \\
&= 2\langle f,g \rangle \quad \text{(assuming real-valued inner product)}
\end{aligned}
$$
Since $f, g \in L^2$, $f+g \in L^2$ (vector space closure). Thus all norm terms ($||f+g||^2, ||f||^2, ||g||^2$) are finite. Consequently, their linear combination $2\langle f,g \rangle$ must be finite, implying the inner product is finite.
