# Chapter 2: A History of VR

---

## Main Idea

- This chapter traces the evolution of virtual reality from ancient "analog" illusions and 19th-century stereoscopes to the development of modern head-mounted displays (HMDs) and the recent consumer resurgence.

- It highlights that while the core goal of capturing the imagination through artificial worlds has existed for millennia, the sophistication of instruments—from the Link Trainer to the Oculus Rift—has significantly advanced the power and ease of creating these experiences.

---

## Key Principles & Guidelines

**Stereoscopy**

- **Definition:** The technique of creating the illusion of depth by presenting slightly different images to the left and right eyes, originally achieved in the 1800s using mirrors (Wheatstone) or lenses (Brewster).

- **Design Implication:** This is the foundational optical principle for modern VR headsets; early devices like the stereoscope proved that the mind can "feel its way" into a picture, establishing the basis for 3D visual immersion.

**Multi-Sensory Immersion (Sensorama)**

- **Definition:** An approach that integrates wide-angle visuals, stereo sound, haptics (vibrations), wind, and aromas to simulate a physical experience.

- **Design Implication:** Immersion is not limited to vision; engaging visceral senses increases the "magic" of the experience. The Sensorama demonstrated that a world-fixed display could effectively transport users by stimulating multiple sensory channels simultaneously.

**The Sword of Damocles (Tracking & CGI)**

- **Definition:** The first head-mounted display system to utilize both head tracking and computer-generated imagery, created by Ivan Sutherland.

- **Design Implication:** This system established the technical blueprint for modern VR (tracking + rendering). The name serves as a metaphor for the responsibility inherent in VR development and the "precarious" nature of early, heavy hardware.

**Telepresence**

- **Definition:** A system where a user's head movements control a remote camera, allowing them to see and experience a distant environment as if they were there (e.g., Philco Headsight).

- **Design Implication:** VR can decouple physical presence from sensory perception, enabling safe interaction in remote or hazardous environments without computer-generated graphics.

**Field of View (FOV)**

- **Definition:** The extent of the observable world that is seen at any given moment; a critical component often lacking in early consumer HMDs.

- **Design Implication:** A wide FOV is essential for the feeling of "presence" and accurate distance judgment. The lack of wide FOV contributed to the "VR Winter," while its reintroduction (via prototypes like the Wide5 and later Oculus) was a key factor in the modern VR resurgence.

## Practical Use

**Simulation and Training**

- The Link Trainer (1928) demonstrated that mechanical simulation could effectively train pilots by reproducing the motion and sensation of flight, proving VR's value in reducing risk and cost for military and professional training.

**Scientific Visualization**

- Systems like the Grope-III allowed researchers to interact with molecular data using haptic feedback, showing that VR provides tangible understanding of abstract scientific concepts.
