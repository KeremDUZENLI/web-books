# Chapter 16: Measuring Sickness

---

## Main Idea

- This chapter outlines the challenges and methods for quantifying VR sickness, noting that it is a polysymptomatic condition with significant individual variability and potential for adaptation.
- It details the primary measurement tools, including the subjective Kennedy Simulator Sickness Questionnaire (SSQ) and objective measures like postural stability tests and physiological monitoring (e.g., heart rate, skin conductance).

---

## Key Principles & Guidelines

**Challenges in Measurement**

- **Definition:** Measuring VR sickness is difficult because symptoms are diverse (polysymptomatic), impacts are often weak and fleeting, and individuals adapt over time (making re-testing difficult).
- **Design Implication:** Researchers often require large sample sizes and between-subject designs to account for high variance and adaptation effects. Maintaining consistent control conditions across broad user groups is a significant hurdle.

**Kennedy Simulator Sickness Questionnaire (SSQ)**

- **Definition:** The standard subjective tool for measuring sickness, derived from Navy simulator data, which asks users to rank 16 symptoms (e.g., eye strain, vertigo, burping) on a 4-point scale.
- **Design Implication:**
    -   **Subscores:** The SSQ provides a total score and three specific subscores: **Oculomotor** (eye strain, headache), **Disorientation** (dizziness, vertigo), and **Nausea** (stomach awareness, salivation).
    -   **Usage:** It is now standard practice to administer the SSQ only *after* exposure to avoid the "nocebo" effect where pre-questionnaires prime users to feel sick.

**Postural Stability Tests**

- **Definition:** Behavioral measures that assess a user's balance, based on the theory that instability precedes sickness.
- **Design Implication:** Tests like the **Sharpened Romberg Stance** (standing heel-to-toe, arms folded, eyes closed/chin up) measure how often a user breaks their stance, providing an objective metric for disorientation and ataxia.

**Physiological Measures**

- **Definition:** Objective biological metrics that change with sickness, such as heart rate, blink rate, EEG activity, skin conductance (sweating), and pallor (skin color).
- **Design Implication:** While objective, these measures often lack consistency in *direction* across individuals (e.g., heart rate might go up for some and down for others). Therefore, measuring absolute changes is recommended over directional trends.

---

## Practical Use

**Diagnostic Utility of Subscales**

- While the SSQ total score indicates overall sickness severity, the subscales (Nausea, Oculomotor, Disorientation) provide diagnostic clues. For example, a high Oculomotor score might suggest issues with IPD or focus, while high Disorientation might suggest latency or horizon issues.

**Baseline Screening**

- Users who are not in their usual state of good health before a VR experience should be excluded from testing and SSQ analysis to prevent skewing the data.
