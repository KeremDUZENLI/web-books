# Chapter 7: Perceptual Models and Processes

---

## Main Idea

- This chapter explores various physiological and psychological models—including afference/efference, iterative processing, and Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP)—to explain how the human brain interprets sensory data.
- It emphasizes that perception is a constructive process where the mind filters and interprets "proximal" stimuli (sensory inputs) based on "top-down" expectations and internal mental models, requiring VR creators to design for both subconscious reflexes and conscious reflection.

---

## Key Principles & Guidelines

**Distal vs. Proximal Stimuli**

- **Definition:** **Distal stimuli** are the actual objects in the objective world; **proximal stimuli** are the energy signals (e.g., retinal images, sound waves) that actually reach the sensory receptors.
- **Design Implication:** VR creators build an artificial distal world and project it as proximal stimuli. The goal is to generate proximal stimuli that match the brain's expectations so the user perceives the intended distal objects rather than the display technology.

**Afference and Efference (Re-afference)**

- **Definition:** **Afference** is sensory input traveling to the brain; **Efference** is the motor command sent to muscles. The brain sends an **efference copy** to predict the resulting sensory input. If the actual input matches the prediction (**re-afference**), the world is perceived as stable.
- **Design Implication:** If a user moves their head (active motion) and the VR visual response lags or doesn't match the expected efference copy, the brain interprets the discrepancy as the world moving (passive motion), often leading to motion sickness.

**Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Processing**

- **Definition:** **Bottom-up** processing is driven by incoming sensory data (pixels, sound); **Top-down** processing is driven by prior knowledge, expectations, and mental models.
- **Design Implication:** VR relies on high-quality bottom-up stimuli (resolution, frame rate) to be convincing enough to overcome the user's top-down knowledge that they are merely wearing a headset.

**Levels of Processing (Norman’s Model)**

- **Definition:** Cognition occurs at four levels:
  - **Visceral:** Immediate, "lizard brain" reflexes (e.g., startle response).
  - **Behavioral:** Learned, subconscious skills (e.g., typing, driving).
  - **Reflective:** Conscious, slow analysis and storytelling.
  - **Emotional:** Affective processing that assigns value and creates memories.
- **Design Implication:** Effective VR must appeal to all levels: aesthetic intuition for visceral impact, intuitive controls for behavioral ease, and coherent narratives for reflective engagement.

**Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP) Filters**

- **Definition:** A model describing how the mind **deletes**, **distorts**, and **generalizes** incoming information based on internal filters like **Meta Programs**, **Values**, **Beliefs**, and **Memories**.
- **Design Implication:** Users perceive the same VR environment differently based on their internal "program." Creators cannot control these filters but can influence them by understanding target personas and providing consistent sensory cues to help users form accurate generalizations.

---

## Practical Use

**Combating Learned Helplessness**

- "Learned helplessness" occurs when a user decides a task is impossible after a few failed attempts, often due to bad interaction design. To prevent this, VR applications must provide immediate, clear feedback (even for failure) to help users adjust their mental models rather than giving up.

**Designing for Preferred Modalities**

- Individuals have dominant sensory modalities (visual, auditory, kinesthetic). A VR experience designed solely around the creator's preference (e.g., purely visual) may fail for users who are kinesthetic learners. Designers should provide redundant cues across multiple senses to ensure comprehension for a wider audience.
