# Chapter 35: The Present and Future State of VR

---

## Main Idea

- This chapter discusses the democratization of VR in 2015, noting that low-cost consumer hardware and accessible development tools have leveled the playing field between indie developers and large corporations.
- It explores the future trajectory of the medium, predicting a shift from selling "technology" to selling compelling experiences, the rise of new communication paradigms (including brain-to-brain interfaces), and the inevitable convergence of AR and VR hardware.

---

## Key Principles & Guidelines

**Democratization and Culture**

- **Definition:** The shift from VR being an academic/corporate tool to a consumer technology where anyone can define the medium's future.
- **Trend:** Grassroots movements like **VR Meetups** (pioneered by Karl Krantz) have proven that VR is a social lubricant, bringing introverts together to share creations, contrary to the stereotype of VR as isolating.

**Communication Paradigms**

- **Generative Language:** VR is described as a "generative language" that creates new futures and transforms perception, rather than just describing existing ones.
- **Symbolic Input:** A major current bottleneck. Replicating 2D desktop widgets (keyboards) in VR is inefficient. Future input may rely on chord keyboards, pinch gloves, or improved gesture recognition, though these have steep learning curves.
- **Empathy Systems:** AI-driven virtual humans (like those from USC ICT) can sense user emotion and respond appropriately, creating rapport that is difficult to achieve with traditional interfaces.
- **Brain-to-Brain:** Proof-of-concept experiments have successfully transmitted "phosphenes" (light flashes) from one human brain to another via EEG and TMS, hinting at a distant future of direct neural communication.

**Standards and Open Source**

- **Role of Standards:** Standards act as tools to simplify communication and allow objective comparison (e.g., defining "latency" consistently).
    -   **Open Source:** Code that is free to use and modify (e.g., VRPN, OSVR).
    -   **De Facto Standards:** Proprietary platforms that dominate by market share (e.g., DirectX).
    -   **Open Standards:** Specifications controlled by non-profit consensus groups with equal voting rights (e.g., Khronos Group, ITA).

**Hardware Evolution**

- **Hand Tracking:** Current input is often limited to "boxing glove" interactions (imprecise). Future controllers and gloves (like the Tyndall/TSSG glove) aim to bring fine motor control and finger tracking to consumers.
- **AR/VR Convergence:** While the experiences differ, the hardware is merging. VR HMDs use cameras for "augmented virtuality," while future AR glasses will use opaque pixels to block the real world, effectively becoming VR displays.

---

## Practical Use

**Selling to the Masses**

- To reach beyond early adopters, VR must stop being sold as a "technical marvel" (resolution, latency) and start being sold on benefits: entertainment, social connection, cost savings, and quality of life improvements.

**The "Boxing Glove" Problem**

- Designers must currently account for the fact that most users lack reliable finger tracking. Interactions should be designed for coarse hand movements (the "boxing glove" metaphor) until high-fidelity gloves become consumer-ready.
