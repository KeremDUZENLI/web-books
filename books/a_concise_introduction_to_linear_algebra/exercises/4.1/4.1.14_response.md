### Answer 4.1.14

**Proof:**
Line $L: \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{v}$.
$T(L) = T(\mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{p}) + tT(\mathbf{v})$.
This is a line through $T(\mathbf{p})$ with direction $T(\mathbf{v})$ (unless $T(\mathbf{v})=\mathbf{0}$, then it's a point).
Two parallel lines: $L_1: \mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{v}$, $L_2: \mathbf{q} + t\mathbf{v}$.
Images: $T(L_1): T(\mathbf{p}) + tT(\mathbf{v})$, $T(L_2): T(\mathbf{q}) + tT(\mathbf{v})$.
Both have direction $T(\mathbf{v})$. Thus, they are parallel (or points).
