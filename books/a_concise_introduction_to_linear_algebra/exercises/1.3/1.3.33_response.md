### Answer 1.3.33

**Given:** $f(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{q} - d$.

**Logic:**
Let $P$ be the orthogonal projection of $Q$ onto the plane $S$.
Then $\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{n}$ for some distance $t$.
$f(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{n} \cdot (\mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{n}) - d$.
$f(\mathbf{q}) = (\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{p}) + t(\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{n}) - d$.
Since $P$ is on the plane, $\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{p} = d$.
Since $\mathbf{n}$ is a unit vector, $\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{n} = 1$.
$f(\mathbf{q}) = d + t - d = t$.
$t$ is exactly the signed distance along the normal vector from the plane to $Q$.
