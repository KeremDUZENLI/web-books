### Answer 3.5.8

**Proof:**
Let $U, V$ be subspaces of $X$.
$U+V = \{ \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \mid \mathbf{u} \in U, \mathbf{v} \in V \}$.
1.  **Zero Vector:** Since $\mathbf{0} \in U$ and $\mathbf{0} \in V$, $\mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0} \in U+V$.
2.  **Closure under Addition:**
    Let $\mathbf{w}_1, \mathbf{w}_2 \in U+V$.
    $\mathbf{w}_1 = \mathbf{u}_1 + \mathbf{v}_1$, $\mathbf{w}_2 = \mathbf{u}_2 + \mathbf{v}_2$.
    $\mathbf{w}_1 + \mathbf{w}_2 = (\mathbf{u}_1 + \mathbf{v}_1) + (\mathbf{u}_2 + \mathbf{v}_2) = (\mathbf{u}_1 + \mathbf{u}_2) + (\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2)$.
    Since $U, V$ are subspaces, $\mathbf{u}_1+\mathbf{u}_2 \in U$ and $\mathbf{v}_1+\mathbf{v}_2 \in V$.
    Thus $\mathbf{w}_1 + \mathbf{w}_2 \in U+V$.
3.  **Closure under Scalar Multiplication:**
    $c\mathbf{w}_1 = c(\mathbf{u}_1 + \mathbf{v}_1) = c\mathbf{u}_1 + c\mathbf{v}_1$.
    Since subspaces are closed under scaling, $c\mathbf{u}_1 \in U$ and $c\mathbf{v}_1 \in V$.
    Thus $c\mathbf{w}_1 \in U+V$.
Therefore, $U+V$ is a subspace.
