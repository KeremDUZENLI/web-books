### Answer 7.2.1

**Proof:**
A diagonalizable matrix $A$ can be written as $A = S \Lambda S^{-1}$, where $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n)$.
The determinant of $A$ is the product of its eigenvalues: $\det(A) = \det(S \Lambda S^{-1}) = \det(\Lambda) = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 \dots \lambda_n$.

1.  **Invertible $\iff$ Non-zero Eigenvalues:**
    $A$ is invertible $\iff \det(A) \neq 0 \iff \prod \lambda_i \neq 0 \iff$ all $\lambda_i \neq 0$.

2.  **Negative Powers:**
    If all $\lambda_i \neq 0$, then $\Lambda$ is invertible with $\Lambda^{-1} = \text{diag}(1/\lambda_1, \dots, 1/\lambda_n)$.
    $$ A^{-1} = (S \Lambda S^{-1})^{-1} = (S^{-1})^{-1} \Lambda^{-1} S^{-1} = S \Lambda^{-1} S^{-1} $$
    By induction for any integer $k > 0$:
    $$ A^{-k} = (A^{-1})^k = S (\Lambda^{-1})^k S^{-1} = S \Lambda^{-k} S^{-1} $$
    Thus, the formula $A^k = S \Lambda^k S^{-1}$ holds for negative integers $k$.
