### Answer 8.3.1

**Matrix:**
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$
Eigenvalues are 3 (multiplicity 2) and 1. Dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 3$.

**a. Power Method Iteration ($k=1$):**
Method: $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = A \mathbf{x}_k$. Normalize if needed (Theorem 8.3.1 typically looks at ratios of components).

1.  **Initial Vector $\mathbf{x}_0 = (1, 1, 1)^T$:**
    $\mathbf{x}_1 = A \mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$.
    Ratios: $x_{1,1}/x_{0,1} = 4/1 = 4$. $x_{1,2}/x_{0,2} = 3/1 = 3$.
    It is converging towards 3, but the first component has contamination from the non-dominant eigenvector due to the structure.
    Wait, eigenvalues are 3, 3, 1. Eigenvectors for $\lambda=3$: $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2$. Eigenvector for $\lambda=1$: $\mathbf{v}_3 = (-1, 0, 2)^T$? Check: $A(-1, 0, 2) = (-3+2, 0, 2) = (-1, 0, 2)$. Yes.
    $\mathbf{x}_0 = (1, 1, 1) = \alpha \mathbf{e}_1 + \beta \mathbf{e}_2 + \gamma \mathbf{v}_3$.
    $(1, 1, 1) = a(1,0,0) + b(0,1,0) + c(-0.5, 0, 1)$?
    Let's just look at the limit. The component in the direction of the dominant eigenspace (span of $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2$) will dominate.
    $\mathbf{x}_k \to c \cdot 3^k$. Ratio $\approx 3$.

2.  **Initial Vector $\mathbf{x}_0 = (1, -1, 1)^T$:**
    $\mathbf{x}_1 = A \mathbf{x}_0 = (4, -3, 1)^T$.
    Ratios: $4/1=4$, $-3/-1=3$, $1/1=1$.

**b. Modification for Multiplicity:**
If $\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 > \dots$, the sequence of vectors $\mathbf{x}_k$ will converge to the subspace spanned by the eigenvectors corresponding to the dominant eigenvalue.
The vector won't settle on a single eigenvector if the starting vector has components of both, but the ratio of norms $\|\mathbf{x}_{k+1}\| / \|\mathbf{x}_k\|$ (or components) will still converge to $\lambda_1$.
No major modification to the algorithm is needed to find the *eigenvalue*, but finding a *specific* eigenvector depends on the starting vector.
