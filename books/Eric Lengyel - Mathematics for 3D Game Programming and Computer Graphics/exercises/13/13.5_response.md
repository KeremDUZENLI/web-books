### Answer 13.5

Let $P = (x_P, 0, z_P)$. The trajectory passes through origin $O$ and $P$.
The straight line $OP$ is given by $z_{line}(x) = \frac{z_P}{x_P} x$.
Projectile path: $z(x) = (\tan \alpha) x - \frac{g x^2}{2 v_0^2 \cos^2 \alpha}$.

**1. Vertical Difference Constraint**
Difference $D(x) = z(x) - z_{line}(x)$.
Max difference is $h$.
This geometric constraint implies specific kinematic relations.
Alternatively, in a coordinate system rotated to align with OP (downrange axis $x'$), the "effective gravity" component perpendicular to OP determines the height $h$.
Let $T$ be time to reach $P$.
Midpoint of time $t = T/2$ is where max deviation $h$ occurs relative to the line.
Deviation $h = \frac{1}{2} g_\perp (T/2)^2$? No, standard projectile deviation from the straight line shooting directly at target is $\frac{1}{2} g t^2$.
So, at midpoint (max deviation), $h = \frac{1}{8} g T^2$? No.

Let's use the property that the vertical distance between the projectile and the line of sight (LOS) grows as $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$.
However, gravity acts downwards, not perpendicular to LOS.
Vertical distance $\Delta z = z_{projectile} - z_{line}$.
$z_{line}(t) = \frac{z_P}{x_P} v_x t$ (assuming constant horizontal velocity $v_x$).
$z_{proj}(t) = v_{z0} t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2$.
$\Delta z(t) = (v_{z0} - \frac{z_P}{x_P} v_x) t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2$.
This is a parabola opening downward. Max height $h$ occurs at vertex.
Vertex time $t_m = \frac{v_{z0} - (z_P/x_P)v_x}{g}$.
Max value $h = \frac{(v_{z0} - (z_P/x_P)v_x)^2}{2g}$.
So, $(v_{z0} - \frac{z_P}{x_P} v_x) = \sqrt{2gh}$.

**2. Hit Constraint**
At time $T$, projectile is at $P(x_P, z_P)$.
$x_P = v_x T$.
$z_P = v_{z0} T - \frac{1}{2} g T^2$.
From $T = x_P / v_x$:
$z_P = v_{z0} \frac{x_P}{v_x} - \frac{g x_P^2}{2 v_x^2}$.
$v_{z0} = \frac{z_P v_x}{x_P} + \frac{g x_P}{2 v_x}$.

**3. Solve for Velocities**
Substitute $v_{z0}$ into the height equation:
$(\frac{z_P v_x}{x_P} + \frac{g x_P}{2 v_x} - \frac{z_P}{x_P} v_x) = \sqrt{2gh}$
$\frac{g x_P}{2 v_x} = \sqrt{2gh}$
$v_x = \frac{g x_P}{2\sqrt{2gh}} = x_P \sqrt{\frac{g}{8h}}$.

Now find $v_{z0}$:
$v_{z0} = \frac{z_P}{x_P} (x_P \sqrt{\frac{g}{8h}}) + \sqrt{2gh}$
$v_{z0} = z_P \sqrt{\frac{g}{8h}} + \sqrt{2gh}$.

**4. Initial Velocity Vector**
$$v_0 = \langle x_P \sqrt{\frac{g}{8h}}, 0, z_P \sqrt{\frac{g}{8h}} + \sqrt{2gh} \rangle$$
